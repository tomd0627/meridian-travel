Featured Itinerary

10 Days in Japan

Tokyo to Kyoto — a journey through the tension between ancient ritual and relentless modernity, distilled into ten extraordinary days.

Day 01

Arrival in Tokyo: Shinjuku and First Impressions

Your journey begins the moment you land. After a private transfer to your Shinjuku hotel, the afternoon is yours — step into the controlled chaos of Kabukicho, find a quiet ramen bar in Golden Gai, or simply let the city find you. Tonight: dinner at a standing sushi counter, the first lesson in how Japan operates at a different frequency.

Day 02

Shibuya, Harajuku, and Omotesando

Start at Shibuya Crossing — nowhere else in the world generates quite this much organised kinetic energy. Move through Harajuku's side streets into the zelkova-lined Omotesando, Tokyo's most architecturally self-conscious boulevard. Lunch at a rooftop terrace in Aoyama; afternoon at the Nezu Museum garden, where stone lanterns stand in deliberate silence.

Day 03

Old Tokyo: Tsukiji, teamLab, and Ginza

Dawn at Tsukiji Outer Market, where the city's relationship with the sea is on unashamed display. Mid-morning: the digital art universe of teamLab Borderless, where the boundary between observer and artwork ceases to exist. Afternoon in Ginza — the Itoya flagship for stationery, the Ginza Six terrace for a vantage point. Evening: a reservation at a ten-seat omakase counter.

Day 04

Day Trip to Nikkō: Gold Leaf and Cedar Avenues

An early Shinkansen north brings you to Nikkō, where the extravagance of the Tōshō-gū shrine complex is unapologetically at odds with the restrained aesthetic of much of Japanese design — and all the more fascinating for it. The approach through ancient cedar avenues is one of Japan's great ceremonial experiences. Return to Tokyo by early evening.

Day 05

Hakone: Ryokan Night and Mount Fuji Views

The romance of Japan lives, in part, in the ryokan experience. Your night in Hakone — tatami rooms, communal onsen fed by volcanic springs, and a multi-course kaiseki dinner that takes three hours and tastes like a manifesto — is the pivot point of the trip. If the weather is kind, Fuji appears across Lake Ashi in a silence that needs no description.

Day 06

Shinkansen to Kyoto: Arashiyama and the Bamboo Grove

The Shinkansen to Kyoto runs so smoothly it feels fictional. Arrive mid-morning and head directly to Arashiyama before the crowds arrive in force. The bamboo grove is ten minutes of near-religious experience — the sound alone, the way light fractures through the canopy — then Tenryū-ji garden, then a slow lunch by the Ōi River. Your Kyoto hotel awaits: a converted machiya townhouse in Higashiyama.

Day 07

Fushimi Inari and Gion at Dusk

Rise before dawn and reach Fushimi Inari at first light, when the thousands of torii gates belong only to you and the mountain. The full ascent takes two hours and rewards with a view across the entire city. Afternoon: Gion's cobblestone lanes as the light turns amber, the possibility of a chance encounter with a maiko on her way to an evening engagement. Dinner at a kappo restaurant overlooking the Kamo River.

Day 08

Nishiki Market, the Philosopher's Path, and Temple-Hopping

Nishiki Market — Kyoto's kitchen — is five alleyway blocks of pickled plum, grilled skewers, tofu shops, and knife vendors that have occupied this space for four centuries. Then: the Philosopher's Path, a canal walkway designed for precisely the kind of unhurried thinking its name implies. Afternoon: Nanzen-ji, Heian Jingū, the Kyoto National Museum. Today is a full immersion in why Kyoto is classified as an open-air museum.

Day 09

Day Trip to Nara: Sacred Deer and Tōdai-ji

A short train journey delivers you to Nara, Japan's first permanent capital, where over a thousand deer wander the parkland as living heritage — considered messengers of the gods, protected by law, entirely unfazed by human presence. At the centre of it all: Tōdai-ji, housing a bronze Buddha fifteen metres tall that has presided over the valley since 752 CE. Return to Kyoto in the late afternoon for a final evening in Gion.

Day 10

Departure: Kyoto Station Farewell

Kyoto Station is, unexpectedly, an architectural event in itself — Hiroshi Hara's steel and glass cathedral to movement, all soaring angles and escalators that lead nowhere in particular and everywhere at once. After a final morning walk through Higashiyama, your private transfer departs for Osaka Kansai International Airport. Japan does not let you go easily. That is, of course, the point.
